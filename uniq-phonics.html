üìö Definition of Terms

Phonics ‚Äì A method of teaching reading and spelling by connecting sounds (phonemes) to written symbols (graphemes).

Phoneme ‚Äì The smallest unit of sound in a word (e.g. /s/, /a/, /t/).

Grapheme ‚Äì The written letter or group of letters representing a sound (e.g. "s," "a," "t").

Blend ‚Äì The process of joining sounds together to form a word (e.g. /s/ + /a/ + /t/ = sat).

Segmenting ‚Äì Breaking a word down into its individual sounds. Example: dog = /d/ + /o/ + /g/

Digraph ‚Äì Two letters that make one sound (e.g. "ch," "sh," "th").

Consonant Cluster ‚Äì Two or more consonant sounds spoken together (e.g. "bl," "str").

Vowel ‚Äì Letters representing open sounds: a, e, i, o, u (and sometimes y).

Short Vowels ‚Äì a (cat), e (bed), i (sit), o (hot), u (cup)

Long Vowels ‚Äì Vowels that say their name. a (cake), e (tree), i (bike), o (hope), u (tube)

CVC Words ‚Äì Consonant-Vowel-Consonant words. Examples: cat, bed, sit

Decodable Word ‚Äì A word that follows standard phonics rules and can be sounded out (e.g. cat, hop, run).

Prefix ‚Äì Letters added to the beginning of a word to change its meaning. Examples: un-, re-, pre-

Suffix ‚Äì Letters added to the end of a word to change its meaning or function. Examples: -ed, -ing, -s

Syllable ‚Äì A unit of pronunciation with one vowel sound. Examples: cat (1), basket (2), wonderful (3)

üìñ Blending and Word Practice Summary

Key Blending Strategies for Teachers:

1

Sound-by-sound blending

Point to each letter and say its sound, then blend smoothly

2

Continuous blending

Say sounds without stopping between them

3

Chunking

Break longer words into manageable parts (con-tent, sun-set)

4

Use hand motions

Slide finger under word as you blend

5

Practice daily

10-15 minutes of blending practice

Progression:

Step 1

CVC words

cat, dog, sun

Consonant-Vowel-Consonant: The simplest word structure

Step 2

CCVC words

stop, glad, trap

Consonant-Consonant-Vowel-Consonant: Adding initial blends

Step 3

CVCC words

fast, send, milk

Consonant-Vowel-Consonant-Consonant: Adding final blends

Step 4

CCVCC words

stamp, trust, plant

Both initial and final blends combined

Step 5

Multi-syllable words

sunset, basket, content

Breaking words into syllables for easier decoding

üìù Spelling Practice

Teaching Strategy: Spelling requires pattern recognition, rules, and memorization. Use multi-sensory approaches.

Common Spelling Rules

Rule 1: I before E except after C

(or when sounding like A as in neighbor)

Examples: believe, piece, field, receive, ceiling, neighbor, weigh

Rule 2: Drop the E before adding -ing or -ed

Examples: make ‚Üí making, bake ‚Üí baked, smile ‚Üí smiling, hope ‚Üí hoped

Rule 3: Change Y to I when adding endings

(if Y follows a consonant)

Examples: happy ‚Üí happiness, baby ‚Üí babies, try ‚Üí tried, study ‚Üí studied

BUT: play ‚Üí playing (Y follows a vowel, so keep the Y)

Rule 4: Double the final consonant

(in one-syllable CVC words before -ing or -ed)

Examples: run ‚Üí running, hop ‚Üí hopping, stop ‚Üí stopped, sit ‚Üí sitting

Rule 5: Use CK after short vowels

Examples: back, duck, sick, lock, truck, black, stick

Rule 6: Use K after long vowels or consonants

Examples: make, bike, book, park, milk, talk

Rule 7: Words ending in -ch, -sh, -ss, -x, or -z add -ES for plurals

Examples: church ‚Üí churches, bush ‚Üí bushes, kiss ‚Üí kisses, box ‚Üí boxes, buzz ‚Üí buzzes

Rule 8: Words ending in consonant + O add -ES for plurals

Examples: hero ‚Üí heroes, potato ‚Üí potatoes, tomato ‚Üí tomatoes, echo ‚Üí echoes

BUT: photo ‚Üí photos, piano ‚Üí pianos (exceptions)

Rule 9: Words ending in F or FE change to -VES for plurals

Examples: wolf ‚Üí wolves, knife ‚Üí knives, shelf ‚Üí shelves, life ‚Üí lives

BUT: roof ‚Üí roofs, chief ‚Üí chiefs (exceptions)

Rule 10: Silent E makes vowels say their name

(long vowel sound)

Examples: cap ‚Üí cape, kit ‚Üí kite, hop ‚Üí hope, tub ‚Üí tube, pin ‚Üí pine

Rule 11: When two vowels go walking, the first one does the talking

Examples: boat, rain, dream, meat, paint, tea, speed, coat

Rule 12: Use -TION after consonants, -SION after vowels or L

-TION: action, invention, reaction (after consonants)

-SION: division, occasion, confusion, expulsion (after vowels/L)

Rule 13: Add -FUL not -FULL

(only one L at the end)

Examples: beautiful, helpful, wonderful, careful, peaceful, grateful

Rule 14: -ABLE vs -IBLE

(use -ABLE if you can remove it and have a complete word)

-ABLE: breakable, enjoyable, comfortable, washable

-IBLE: terrible, horrible, possible, visible

Rule 15: Q is always followed by U

Examples: queen, question, quiet, quick, quit, quilt, square, equal

üîç Prefixes and Suffixes

Teaching Strategy: Prefixes and suffixes are morphemes‚Äîmeaningful word parts added to the beginning or end of a base word to change its meaning, tense, number, or grammatical function. Teach them as "word chunks" that help students decode unfamiliar words, expand vocabulary, improve spelling, and strengthen reading comprehension.

Encourage learners to:

  • Identify the base word
  • Identify the prefix or suffix
  • Combine the meanings to understand the whole word

Common Prefixes with Meanings and Examples

1. un- (not, opposite of)

unhappy, unkind, unfair, unsafe, untidy, unlock, unpack, unreal, unsure

"un-" makes the base word negative.

2. re- (again, back)

redo, replay, rewrite, return, rebuild, rethink, reheat, review, refill

Often used with verbs to show repetition.

3. pre- (before)

preview, preheat, prefix, preschool, prepay, pre-order, pretest, prearrange

Helps predict sequence in texts.

4. dis- (not, opposite of, reverse)

dislike, disagree, disconnect, disappear, disapprove, disobey, dishonest

Useful for forming antonyms.

5. mis- (wrongly, badly)

mistake, misplace, misbehave, mislead, misread, misspell, misjudge, misinterpret

Helps learners detect incorrect actions.

6. in-/im-/il-/ir- (not)

incomplete, incorrect, inactive, impossible, impatient, impolite, illegal, illegible, irregular, irresponsible, irresistible

Spelling note: im- before m, p, b | il- before l | ir- before r

7. over- (too much, above)

overeat, overwork, overcook, overheat, oversleep, overreact, overconfident

Warning for exaggeration or excess.

8. under- (too little, below)

undercook, underestimate, underwater, underpay, underdeveloped, underline, undercover

Shows insufficiency or lower position.

9. anti- (against, preventing)

antibiotic, antifreeze, anti-social, anti-clockwise, anti-virus

Common in science vocabulary.

10. inter- (between, among)

international, interact, internet, interschool, intercity, interchange

Shows connection or relationship.

11. trans- (across, through)

transport, transfer, translate, transcontinental, transaction

Useful for geography and science.

12. post- (after)

postpone, postwar, postgraduate, post-test

Shows sequence or order.

13. sub- (under, below)

subway, submarine, subheading, subdivide, subconscious

Opposite of "super-".

14. super- (above, beyond, very)

superhero, superhuman, superb, supermarket, superstar

Shows something greater than normal.

Common Suffixes with Meanings and Examples

1. -s / -es (plural or third-person singular)

cats, books, dogs, watches, buses, runs, plays, dances

2. -ed (past tense)

walked, jumped, played, laughed, travelled

3. -ing (continuous action)

walking, reading, swimming, dancing

4. -er (person who, or comparison)

Person: teacher, runner, singer
Comparison: smaller, taller, faster

5. -est (superlative)

biggest, tallest, smartest, strongest

6. -ful (full of)

beautiful, helpful, careful, joyful, thankful

7. -less (without)

hopeless, powerless, careless, fearless, homeless

8. -ness (state or quality)

kindness, darkness, happiness, weakness, sadness

9. -able / -ible (can be, able to be)

readable, washable, breakable, visible, flexible, accessible

Spelling note: -ible mainly used with Latin roots.

10. -ment (action or process)

enjoyment, improvement, development, payment, movement

11. -tion / -sion / -cian (state, quality; makes nouns)

action, vacation, education, discussion, revision, explosion, musician, electrician, physician

Common in academic vocabulary.

12. -ly (in a manner; forms adverbs)

quickly, slowly, quietly, happily, bravely

13. -ous / -ious (full of, having the quality of)

dangerous, famous, nervous, curious, delicious, ambitious

14. -al (relating to)

natural, national, personal, cultural, physical

15. -ive (tending toward, having nature of)

creative, active, imaginative, responsive

16. -en (to make; or made of)

To make: harden, shorten, widen, strengthen
Made of: wooden, golden
